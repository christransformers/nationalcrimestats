NSW and QLD — why we focus on these two states

Succession law in Australia is state-based. Each state has its own legislation, its own court procedures, and its own body of case law. While the underlying principles — testamentary capacity, undue influence, the proper administration of estates — are broadly similar across jurisdictions, the detail matters. Limitation periods differ. Family provision eligibility differs. Procedural rules differ.

By focusing on NSW and Queensland, we ensure that we are deeply familiar with both the Succession Act 2006 (NSW) and the Succession Act 1981 (QLD), as well as the relevant court rules and tribunal jurisdictions (NCAT in NSW, QCAT in Queensland). This focus means we are not spreading our attention across six or more state and territory systems — we know the two that matter for our clients.

Phone and video consultations — how they work

We have invested in making our remote consultation process seamless. Here is how it works:

  1. Book a time. Contact us by phone or through our enquiry form. We will arrange a consultation at a time that suits you — including outside standard business hours where necessary.
  2. Send documents (optional). If you have relevant documents — a will, correspondence, financial records — you can send them to us securely before the consultation. This allows us to provide more specific advice.
  3. The consultation. By phone or video, we will listen to your situation, ask questions to clarify the facts, and provide an initial assessment of your legal position. We will also discuss likely next steps, costs, and timeframes.
  4. Written summary. After the consultation, we can provide a brief written summary of the advice given and the recommended next steps.

Cross-border matters

If the deceased owned property in both NSW and Queensland, or if relevant conduct occurred across the border, we can manage both aspects of the matter. Probate is typically sought in the state where the principal assets are located, with a reseal in the other state. Our familiarity with both jurisdictions means we can handle cross-border estates without needing to instruct separate lawyers in each state.
