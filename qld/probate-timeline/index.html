QLD Probate Timeline — How Long Each Stage Takes & What to Expect

Probate in Queensland is a multi-stage process governed by the Succession Act 1981 (QLD) and the Uniform Civil Procedure Rules 1999 (QLD). From locating the will to final distribution, the process typically takes 10 to 14 weeks in straightforward cases — but delays, disputes, and complex estates can extend it substantially. This guide walks you through each stage, explains what happens and when, and identifies the factors that most commonly cause delay. Understanding the timeline helps both executors and beneficiaries know what to expect and when to seek legal advice.

Overview: The QLD probate timeline at a glance

Below is a visual summary of the six key stages in a standard Queensland probate — from the date of death through to estate distribution. Each stage is explained in detail in the sections that follow.

Stage 1: Locate Will

Weeks 1–2. Locate the original will, secure assets, obtain death certificate, notify key institutions. Initial executor steps.

Stage 2: Prepare Application

Weeks 2–8. Gather asset and liability information, value the estate, prepare affidavits, draft probate application and supporting documents.

Stage 3: File & Advertise

Weeks 8–10. File application with Supreme Court. Publish mandatory probate advertisement. Serve notice on the Public Trustee.

Stage 4: Notice Period

14 + 14 days. Mandatory 14-day advertising period. The Public Trustee has a 14-day notice period. Both run concurrently in practice.

Stage 5: Grant Issued

Weeks 10–12+. Supreme Court reviews the application. If all requirements are met, the grant of probate is sealed. Executor now has legal authority.

Stage 6: Administer & Distribute

Weeks 12+. Call in assets, pay debts and tax, attend to any claims, distribute the estate to beneficiaries. Duration depends on complexity.

Stage 1: Locating the will and initial steps (weeks 1–2)

The first stage begins immediately upon death. The executor's priority is to locate the original will — a copy is not sufficient for a probate application in Queensland. The will may be held by the deceased's solicitor, in a safe custody facility with a bank, in a personal safe at home, or lodged with the Supreme Court of Queensland for safekeeping. If the location is unknown, check with the deceased's solicitor, accountant, financial adviser, and close family members.

During these first two weeks, the executor should also:

  • Secure the deceased's assets. Lock and secure any vacant real property. Notify banks and financial institutions of the death (accounts will be frozen until probate is granted). Redirect mail. Ensure appropriate insurance is maintained on all property.
  • Arrange the funeral. The executor ordinarily has the right to make funeral arrangements, even before probate is granted. Funeral expenses are a first charge on the estate.
  • Obtain the death certificate. Apply through Births, Deaths and Marriages Queensland. The funeral director usually assists with this process. The death certificate is required for the probate application.
  • Identify beneficiaries and potential claimants. Review the will to identify all beneficiaries. Consider who else might have an interest — spouses, children, dependants, and any person who might bring a family provision claim under Part 4 of the Succession Act 1981 (QLD).
  • Notify beneficiaries. While there is no statutory obligation to notify beneficiaries immediately, it is good practice to inform the named beneficiaries that the will has been located and that you intend to apply for probate. Transparency early can reduce suspicion and future disputes.
  • Check the will for QLD-specific execution issues. Section 10(2) of the Succession Act 1981 (QLD) requires the testator's signature to appear at the foot or end of the will. If the signature is in an unusual position, legal advice should be sought — a s 18 dispensing application may be needed before probate can be granted.

Stage 2: Preparing the probate application (weeks 2–8)

This is typically the longest stage. The executor (or their solicitor) must prepare the probate application and all supporting documents for filing with the Supreme Court of Queensland. The time required depends heavily on the complexity of the estate, the cooperation of financial institutions, and whether professional valuations are needed.

Key tasks during this stage include:

  • Identify and value all estate assets and liabilities. This includes real property (obtain a market appraisal or formal valuation), bank accounts (request date-of-death balances from each institution), shares and managed funds (obtain holding statements and date-of-death valuations), motor vehicles, personal effects, business interests, and any other assets. On the liabilities side: mortgages, credit cards, personal loans, tax liabilities, and unpaid bills.
  • Prepare the executor's affidavit. This is the central document in the probate application. The affidavit must set out the deceased's details, the will's execution details, the executor's relationship to the deceased, the assets and liabilities of the estate (gross and net values), and confirm that the will is the deceased's last will. The affidavit must be sworn or affirmed before an authorised witness (a Justice of the Peace, solicitor, or barrister).
  • Prepare the inventory of assets and liabilities. Filed with the executor's affidavit, this inventory provides a detailed schedule of the estate's financial position.
  • Lodge the original will. The original will must be lodged with the Court. It should not be marked, stapled, or damaged. If the will has been altered or marked, an additional affidavit explaining the alterations may be required.
  • Arrange for surety (if applicable). In most cases, the Court does not require a surety (a financial guarantee). However, if the executor lives outside Queensland or Australia, or if there are unusual circumstances, the Court may require an administration bond under s 50 of the Succession Act 1981 (QLD).
  • Prepare the probate advertisement. The advertisement must be published in a newspaper circulating in the area where the deceased lived. It must state the deceased's name, date of death, and that an application for probate is being made, and give notice that any person wishing to object must do so within 14 days.

Delay at this stage is common — plan accordingly

Financial institutions can take weeks to provide date-of-death balances and holding statements. Property valuations, particularly for regional or unique properties, may take 2–4 weeks. If the executor delays in gathering this information, or if the estate is complex (multiple properties, overseas assets, business interests), Stage 2 can extend well beyond 8 weeks. Stage 3: Filing and advertising (weeks 8–10)

Once the probate application and supporting documents are complete, the executor files the application with the Supreme Court of Queensland Probate Registry. At this stage, two things happen concurrently:

  • The probate application is filed. The application, executor's affidavit, inventory of assets and liabilities, original will, and any other required documents are lodged with the Probate Registry. A filing fee is payable (fee amounts are set by regulation and subject to change — check the current Supreme Court fee schedule). The Registry will review the documents for completeness and may raise requisitions (requests for further information or clarification) if anything is incomplete or unclear. Responding to requisitions can add 1–2 weeks.
  • The probate advertisement is published. The executor must arrange for the probate advertisement to be published in an approved newspaper. In Queensland, the advertisement must run for at least 14 days. The advertisement invites any person with an objection to the grant of probate to come forward. This is a mandatory step — probate cannot be granted without it.
  • Notice to the Public Trustee of Queensland. Under r 600 of the Uniform Civil Procedure Rules 1999 (QLD), the executor must serve a copy of the probate application on the Public Trustee of Queensland. The Public Trustee has 14 days from service to respond or file any objection. This notice period runs concurrently with the advertising period in practice.

Stage 4: The probate notice period (14 days mandatory advertising + 14 day notice period)

Stage 4 is the mandatory waiting period. During these 14 days (which run concurrently — not sequentially — for the advertisement and the Public Trustee notice), the application sits with the Probate Registry while the community and the Public Trustee have the opportunity to raise any objections or concerns.

Mandatory Advertising Period — 14 Days

The probate advertisement must be published in a newspaper circulating in the area where the deceased resided at the time of death. The notice invites any person who wishes to object to the grant of probate to do so. If no objections are received within the 14-day period, this requirement is satisfied. The advertisement serves a dual purpose: it protects the executor by providing public notice of the application, and it gives potential claimants (creditors, dependants, persons with caveats) an opportunity to come forward.

Public Trustee Notice Period — 14 Days

Under r 600 of the Uniform Civil Procedure Rules 1999 (QLD), the Public Trustee must be served with a copy of the probate application. The Public Trustee has 14 days to consider whether to object or to apply for an order that it be joined. In most routine applications, the Public Trustee does not respond, and the 14 days pass without action. However, the Public Trustee may intervene if it becomes aware of vulnerable beneficiaries, concerns about the executor's suitability, or other issues that warrant scrutiny.

In practice, these two 14-day periods run concurrently, not one after the other. The combined notice period is therefore 14 days, not 28 days — though it may be prudent to allow a few extra days for administrative processing. The Probate Registry will not seal the grant until both periods have expired and no objections have been received.

Objections during the notice period

If a person wishes to object to the grant of probate — for example, by lodging a caveat — they must do so before the grant is sealed. Once probate is granted, the opportunity to prevent the grant without court intervention is lost. If you have concerns about the validity of a will or the executor's conduct, lodge a caveat or obtain legal advice immediately. You can withdraw a caveat later — but you cannot lodge it after the grant. More on caveats in QLD →

Stage 5: Grant issued (weeks 10–12+)

After the notice period expires and the Probate Registry is satisfied that the application is in order, the grant of probate is sealed. This is the formal document issued by the Supreme Court of Queensland confirming the validity of the will and the executor's authority to administer the estate.

The grant of probate is the key document that unlocks the estate. With it, the executor can:

  • Deal with real property. Transmit the deceased's real property to the executor's name (or directly to beneficiaries, depending on the will's terms) by lodging the grant with the Titles Registry. The grant is evidence of the executor's authority to deal with the land.
  • Access bank accounts and financial assets. Provide the grant to banks, share registries, and other financial institutions to close accounts, transfer funds, and sell or transfer securities. Until the grant is produced, financial institutions will not release assets (except for limited purposes such as paying funeral expenses directly).
  • Collect debts owed to the estate. Pursue any debts owing to the deceased and receive payment into the estate.
  • Sell estate assets. List and sell property, shares, vehicles, or other assets necessary to pay debts or to give effect to the will (including where the will directs sale or where assets are to be divided between multiple beneficiaries).

How long does it take for the grant to be issued? In a straightforward, uncomplicated application with no requisitions and no objections, the grant may be sealed within 2–3 weeks of filing. However, if the Probate Registry raises requisitions (requests for further information, correction of errors, or additional documents), the process can take longer — each round of requisitions adds 1–2 weeks. Applications filed during peak periods (or holiday seasons) may also face longer processing times.

Stage 6: Estate administration and distribution

Obtaining the grant of probate is not the end of the process — it is the gateway to estate administration. Stage 6 covers the period from grant to final distribution, and its duration varies widely depending on the nature of the assets, the presence of disputes, and the executor's diligence.

Key tasks after the grant is issued:

  • Call in and liquidate assets. Close bank accounts, sell shares, transfer property titles, collect insurance proceeds, and realise any other assets. Selling real property (particularly in a slow market) and dealing with overseas assets can add months.
  • Pay all debts and liabilities. Pay funeral expenses, outstanding debts, tax liabilities (including the deceased's final income tax return and any capital gains tax arising from asset sales), and the costs of administering the estate (legal fees, valuation fees, advertising costs).
  • Attend to tax obligations. Lodge the deceased's outstanding tax returns and the estate's tax returns. The executor should consider obtaining a tax clearance from the ATO before distribution. An executor who distributes without attending to tax may be personally liable for unpaid amounts.
  • Advertise for claims under s 67 of the Trusts Act 1973 (QLD). This is a separate advertisement from the probate notice — it invites creditors and other claimants to come forward within a specified period (typically 30 days or more). Distributing after the notice period expires, and without notice of any claims, protects the executor from personal liability for claims that emerge later. This is strongly recommended even for estates that appear to have no debts.
  • Consider family provision limitation periods. Under Part 4 of the Succession Act 1981 (QLD), a family provision application must generally be made within 9 months of the date of death (the Court has discretion to extend). An executor who distributes within 9 months does so at their own risk — if a family provision claim is later brought, the executor may be personally liable if the distributed assets cannot be recovered.
  • Distribute the estate to beneficiaries. Once all debts, tax, claims, and limitation periods have been addressed, the executor distributes the remaining assets to the beneficiaries in accordance with the will. This may involve transferring assets in specie (as distinct items) or selling assets and distributing cash. A final set of estate accounts should be prepared and provided to beneficiaries.

Factors that delay probate in Queensland

Not all probates proceed smoothly. Common causes of delay in QLD include:

Cannot locate the original will

If the original will cannot be found, probate cannot be granted in common form. The executor may need to apply for probate of a copy will (requiring evidence that the original was not revoked), or the estate may need to proceed as an intestacy. Either path adds significant time and complexity.

Will execution defects (s 10(2))

Queensland's s 10(2) positional signature rule is unique. If the signature is not at the foot of the will, the will may be defective. A s 18 dispensing application may be required — adding weeks or months and requiring affidavit evidence from witnesses.

Disputes among beneficiaries or executors

Disagreements between co-executors, or between executors and beneficiaries, can stall the administration. If the dispute cannot be resolved, court intervention may be needed — adding months and significant legal costs.

Caveats and objections

A caveat lodged before the grant is sealed stops the probate process in its tracks. The caveator must be given an opportunity to be heard. Resolving a caveat — whether by negotiation, withdrawal, or court determination — can add weeks or months.

Complex asset structures

Estates with business interests, trusts, self-managed super funds, overseas assets, or multiple properties can take substantially longer to value, deal with, and distribute. Tax issues (particularly CGT and trust distributions) add further complexity.

Executor inaction or inexperience

A lay executor who procrastinates, lacks the time to attend to the administration, or simply does not understand what is required can cause significant delay. Beneficiaries frustrated by inaction may apply to the Court for orders compelling the executor to proceed or for the executor to be removed.

Urgent administration options in Queensland

In some circumstances, waiting for a full grant of probate is not practical. The estate may have urgent needs — a business that must continue trading, perishable assets, or a property that must be sold before the market turns. Queensland law provides several mechanisms for dealing with urgent situations:

  • Letters of administration pendente lite (while litigation is pending). Where proceedings are on foot — for example, a will challenge or a dispute about who is entitled to a grant — the Court may appoint an administrator to manage the estate on an interim basis until the proceedings are resolved. The administrator's powers are limited to preserving the estate and are defined by the Court's order.
  • Limited grants of administration. The Court may make a limited grant — for example, limited to a specific asset or for a specific purpose. This might be appropriate where the executor cannot be located immediately but a particular asset must be dealt with urgently.
  • Grants ad colligenda bona (to collect assets). Where estate assets are at risk — for example, a business that needs an authorised person to make urgent decisions, or perishable goods — the Court may appoint an administrator with limited powers to collect and preserve the assets pending a full grant.
  • Interim orders before probate. In some cases, the Court may make orders to protect estate assets before any grant is made — for example, freezing orders to prevent dissipation, or orders for the preservation of evidence. These are exceptional remedies and require strong evidence of urgency and risk.
  • Public Trustee of Queensland intervention. Where no other person is willing or able to act urgently, the Public Trustee may be appointed as administrator of the estate. This can provide a practical solution where there is an impasse or where immediate administration is required.

If you face an urgent estate administration issue, seek legal advice immediately. The longer you wait, the more limited your options may become.

What happens when probate is contested in Queensland

Contested probate — where a person challenges the validity of the will itself, or the executor's entitlement to a grant — can derail the timeline completely. The probate process pauses while the contest is resolved, which can take months or, in some cases, years.

Common grounds for contesting probate in Queensland include:

  • Lack of testamentary capacity. The person challenging the will argues that the deceased did not have the mental capacity to make a valid will at the time of execution. This requires medical evidence and often involves examination of the deceased's treating doctors.
  • Undue influence. The will was made as a result of coercion by another person. The person alleging undue influence carries the burden of proof and must show that the deceased's free will was overborne.
  • Lack of knowledge and approval. The deceased did not truly know and approve the contents of the will — for example, because they were misled about what the will contained, or because they were not given an adequate explanation in circumstances where one was required.
  • Fraud or forgery. The will is a forgery, or the deceased's signature was obtained by fraud. This is a serious allegation that must be supported by evidence.
  • Formal invalidity (s 10 execution defects). The will does not comply with the formal requirements for execution — for example, it was not properly witnessed, or the signature does not comply with the s 10(2) positional requirement. In these cases, the party seeking to propound the will may need to rely on a s 18 dispensing application.
  • Revocation. A later will or other act of revocation means the will being propounded is no longer valid. A later marriage revokes a will unless the will was made in contemplation of that marriage (ss 16 and 17 of the Succession Act 1981).

When probate is contested, the Court will typically require the matter to proceed as a contested hearing. The parties will exchange evidence (affidavits), the matter will be listed for directions, and ultimately a trial will determine the validity of the will and who is entitled to a grant. During this period, the estate may be administered on an interim basis (letters of administration pendente lite), or the parties may agree on an administrator pending the outcome. The costs of contested probate proceedings are usually significant and may be ordered against the unsuccessful party — or, in some cases, paid from the estate.

Differences from the NSW probate timeline

While Queensland and New South Wales probate processes share many common features, there are several important differences that executors and beneficiaries should understand:

6-month probate expectation (NSW only)

NSW has a well-known administrative expectation that probate should be applied for within 6 months of death. An application filed after 6 months must explain the delay. QLD has no equivalent rule. While unreasonable delay in QLD is still problematic and can lead to applications to compel or remove the executor, there is no formal 6-month benchmark against which the Court measures delay.

Family provision limitation period: 9 months (QLD) vs 12 months (NSW)

QLD: 9 months from date of death (s 41(8) Succession Act 1981 QLD). NSW: 12 months from date of death (s 58(2) Succession Act 2006 NSW). In both states, the Court has a discretion to extend time, but the shorter QLD period means applicants must act sooner. Executors in QLD should be aware that the 9-month window closes 3 months earlier than in NSW.

Section 10(2) signature rule (QLD-specific)

Queensland's s 10(2) requirement that the testator's signature be at the "foot or end" of the will is unique. NSW has no equivalent formal requirement. This means QLD executors must check the will's execution position as a matter of course — a step that NSW executors do not need to take. A defective position can require a s 18 dispensing application, adding time and cost.

Public Trustee notice (QLD-specific)

In QLD, the executor must serve a copy of the probate application on the Public Trustee of Queensland (r 600 UCPR). The Public Trustee has 14 days to respond. NSW has no equivalent requirement — the NSW Trustee and Guardian does not receive automatic notice of probate applications.

Advertising period: 14 days (QLD) vs 30 days (NSW)

QLD's mandatory probate advertising period is 14 days. Under NSW practice, notice periods under s 60 of the Trustee Act 1925 (NSW) are typically 30 days. The QLD period is shorter, meaning grants can potentially be sealed sooner in QLD if all other requirements are met.

Caveat procedure

Both states allow caveats against grants of probate, but the procedures differ slightly. In QLD, a caveat is filed in the Supreme Court Probate Registry and must state the grounds of objection. In NSW, caveats are also filed in the Supreme Court Probate Registry. In both states, a caveat must be lodged before the grant is sealed — there is no opportunity to lodge after the grant.

What to do while waiting for probate in Queensland

The period between lodging the probate application and the grant being sealed — and indeed the period between grant and final distribution — can be frustrating for beneficiaries and challenging for executors. Here is what you can and should do while waiting:

For executors

  • Communicate regularly with beneficiaries. Keep beneficiaries informed of progress. A brief update every few weeks — even if there is nothing new to report — can prevent suspicion, complaints, and costly court applications.
  • Continue to secure and maintain assets. Ensure insurance is maintained, properties are secure, and ongoing expenses (rates, body corporate fees, utility connection charges) are attended to. These expenses are estate liabilities.
  • Begin gathering information for the next stage. While waiting for the grant, start compiling the information you will need for administration: contact details for banks and financial institutions, property title references, share registry details, and tax file numbers.
  • Keep detailed records. Record all time spent, expenses incurred, and decisions made. These records are your protection if your actions are later questioned.
  • Do not distribute assets prematurely. Do not make distributions (even partial or informal distributions) until probate is granted, all debts and tax have been dealt with, and any applicable limitation periods have expired. Premature distribution is the most common source of executor personal liability.

For beneficiaries

  • Be patient — but not passive. Probate takes time. In a straightforward QLD estate, expect 10–14 weeks from death to grant, and longer to final distribution. However, if months pass with no communication from the executor, you are entitled to make inquiries.
  • Ask for updates. Write to the executor (politely and in writing) asking for an update on the progress of the administration. Most executors will respond. Keep copies of all correspondence.
  • Know your limitation period. If you are considering a family provision claim, the 9-month limitation period runs from the date of death — not from the date of probate. Do not wait for probate to be granted before seeking legal advice. You need to act before the 9-month window closes.
  • Consider whether a caveat is needed. If you have concerns about the will's validity or the executor's conduct, a caveat must be lodged before probate is granted. Seek legal advice early — waiting to see what happens can be fatal to your position.
  • Seek legal advice if you have concerns. If the executor is not communicating, appears to be acting improperly, or is unreasonably delaying, obtain legal advice. Early intervention is often less costly and more effective than attempting to undo damage after the fact.
